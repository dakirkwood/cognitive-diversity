# ADHD Profile — Attention & Executive Function

## Background

28, variable tech comfort depending on the day and context. Highly capable but easily derailed by environmental noise or unclear next steps. Works best with clear visual hierarchy and minimal distractions. Struggles to re-engage after losing the thread of a multi-step process.

## Cognitive Profile

**Profile:** ADHD (inattentive/combined presentation approximation)

Characterized by attention regulation challenges and executive function differences. Easily distracted by salient but off-task elements (banners, alerts, related links). Struggles to hold multi-step instructions in working memory. Needs clear visual hierarchy to know where to look next. May hyperfocus on an interesting but irrelevant detail. Task completion drops significantly when steps require switching between information sources.

**This is not a personality trait** — it's a neurological processing pattern. Symptoms are highly context-dependent and vary with interest, urgency, and distraction load.

## Navigation Behavior

- Attracted to visually prominent elements even when off-task (banners, notifications, bold callouts)
- Loses thread of multi-step processes after any interruption or page transition
- Skips walls of text — relies on visual hierarchy to understand what to do next
- May start the correct path, get distracted by a related link, and lose the original goal
- Benefits from progress indicators that remind them where they are in a flow
- Performs better with one-thing-per-screen layouts than information-dense pages
- May click "back" and start over if they lose track, rather than reconstructing state

## Friction Triggers

- Multi-column layouts with competing visual weight
- Banners, popups, or interstitials that appear mid-task
- Long paragraphs of instructions with no visual breaks
- Tasks requiring memory across page transitions (e.g., "note the code on this page, then enter it on the next")
- No visible progress indicator in multi-step flows
- Pages where the primary action is not immediately obvious
- Error states that require re-reading long content to understand what went wrong

## Confidence Signals

- Strong visual hierarchy with clear primary action
- Progress indicators visible at all times during multi-step flows
- Single focused task per screen
- Chunked instructions (short steps, not paragraphs)
- Persistent reminder of the goal or current task state

## Reporting Voice

Honest about distraction in a matter-of-fact way. Notes what derailed them without self-criticism. Uses phrases like "I got pulled toward..." and "I lost track of where I was" and "I had to start over because..." Also notices and calls out what helped them stay on track.
